A Quick Primer on Opioid Use Disorder (OUD) and its Treatment


  • OUD results from the misuse of opioids: oxycontin, vicodin, percocet, heroin, dilaudid, etc.
  • OUD is treated with "softer" forms of opioids: methadone, buprenorhpine, and naltrexone. There are >10 FDA-approved medications for OUD.
  • OUD is considered a lifelong illness by the American Society of Addiction Medicine. Patients are advised to continue with medication-assisted treatment for life.
  • Insurers have not covered addiction-related illness and its associated pharmacy cost until the Mental Health Parity Act of 1996 and extension in the Affordable Care Act.
  • Insurers reimburse healthcare providers based on federal and state guidelines, as well as insurer-provider individual agreements.
  • Insurance claims frequently result in provider-insurance company arbitration. Reimbursement can take 3-12 months.
  • Drug abuse is the leading cause of accidental death in the US.
  • Prevalence of OUD is estimated to be 1% in the commercial insurance population and 2-4% in the Medicaid population.
  • Physicians need a special license from the DEA to prescribe methadone and buprenorphine.
  • Stark Business Model differences exist between Methadone and Buprenorphine.
  • Most drug addiction treatment centers do not accept insurance.
